  • Compensation Costs = (Defective × Per Owner) + (Accidents × Per Accident)

    • Without apology: 50% of cost (middling to ignore responsibility)
    • NOTE: This is based off studies that show apologies affect stock positively if receive positive media attention, and negatively if little or negative media attention

  • Plaintiffs get 70% of monetary compensation

Tactics & Strategy

Shell Tactics

  • Be Prepared: Complete EV analysis and "game tape" on Davis Parker's past cases
  • Aim High: Aggressive goals lead to better outcomes; anchor strongly, but defensibly
  • Consistency Princple: Get them to agree first with small things ("were all 111k Hydra accidents because of the pawls?"), then go for more ("how do you expect me to reimburse all of them, then?")

Voss Tactics

  • Tactical Empathy: Listen actively, acknowledge the legitimacy of customer concerns
  • Calibrated Questions: Use thought-provoking How/What questions, ex: "How would a high-profile trial help your clients?", to make them solve our problem
  • "That's Right" Moment: Get Davis to acknowledge the benefits of quick resolution

Cialdini Principles

  • Reciprocity: Please, let me pour you a drink.
  • Unity: How 'bout dem Mets?
  • Scarcity: If we don't reach an agreement soon, someone else could start a class-action against us at any time, and you'd start splitting the client pool.

Anchor and Concessions Plan

  1. Initial anchor: Agree to recall + $70M compensation fund for verified damages
  2. First concession: Add loaner vehicles for 3 days maximum
  3. Second concession: Increase compensation to $350M
  4. Third concession: Public statement acknowledging issue (not a full apology)
  5. Final position: $580M-700M compensation + full recall with loaners + public apology (contingent on favorable comment)

Key Negotiation Insights

1. Don't insult your counterparty.

If you do, make it up by offering them a beer and clinking to the Mets. In all seriousness, negotiations can be tense and fraught with opportunity for accidental insults. Getting to know the other party first and applying Cialdini's principles can raise your EV, make negotiating a more pleasant experience, and build long-term trust.

2. Always seek out integration (always.)

I was surprised by how many integrative (and value destroying!) opportunities I could find in this negotiation. The way I modeled the EV for each side, at best they are able to create about $300M of value and expand the ZOPA significantly. For instance, a public apology accepted graciously by Parker was worth $10M to Parker, $10M to plaintiffs, and was $90M better than PR damage from trial for Payne. On the flip side, issuing a statement absolving Payne of responsibility in the crash which caused the wildfire would be good for Payne's short-term PR crisis (say, $30M) but extremely bad for the lawyer's credibility (-$100M).
